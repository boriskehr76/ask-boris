Being a designer is not a joke

Created on 2019-11-28 10:49

Published on 2019-11-28 11:11

How many designers does it take to change a light bulb?

- Does it have to be a light bulb?

Have you heard it before? Many of my (non designer) colleagues laughed with recognition. They see designers as children with crayons, drawing pretty pictures, asking silly questions and don’t really producing anything real. When confronted with a single, mundane task, designers get stuck. And the joke visualize that in a very striking and funny way. 

I have a totally different interpretation of that joke, and unfortunately it’s not funny at all. Sorry. To me it's the definition of being a designer and design thinker. We designers avoid doings things on autopilot, without reflecting over the purpose. We start by asking questions: "Can it be done in a different, more efficient, accessible, sustainable or fun way? What is the purpose of changing the bulb? Who is going to use it, when, how and why? What kind of light should the bulb produce? Can it do anything more, like change color, intensity or blink? Should it behave differently in the morning, evening, during the night or when nobody is at home? Are there any other possible sources of light?

This base of understanding is source of our inspiration. Then we start prototyping, testing and observing. Without understanding the problem we can not evaluate the result. This is the power of design. To push thing forward and open new possibilities where others see a single solution.

Finding answers to all those question is fascinating, inspiring and drives innovation. But it takes time. Because time is a limited resource, people and companies don’t want to waste it. Your clients wants to see result and final art screenshots, not personas and post-its on a whiteboard. Your jobb is to educate, communicate and involve your boss, clients and colleagues in your design process. Show that those seemingly non productive actions are a vital part of the final solution. Make them co-creators and a part of the process. 

The goal is to make them to ask the question: Does it have to be a light bulb?
