Part 2: Everyone knows it's broken. Nobody owns the fix.

Created on 2026-04-22 15:49

Published on 2026-04-22 15:59

There is no single villain here. No one bad policy, no one bad decision. The hiring system stays broken partly because the pain isn't distributed evenly.

The failure lands in different places

For candidates, it fails loudly. Silence where a decision should be. Rejection templates that say nothing. An invisible bar that keeps moving. For companies, it fails softly. When great candidates fall through the cracks, that loss rarely gets attributed to "the hiring interface." It gets filed under the market, salary expectations, employer brand, senior talent shortage. Problems that live somewhere else, owned by someone else. The people with the power to redesign the process rarely feel the urgency to do so.

Volume looks like opportunity until it becomes noise

More applicants sounds like a feature. More choice should mean better matching. But when 200 to 500 people apply fast, many with AI assistance, hiring stops being evaluation and becomes throughput. Criteria quietly shift: job titles, years of experience, brand-name companies, keyword matches. Shallow filters aren't what anyone wants. They're what scale demands.

The job spec is often the first thing that breaks

Several of the recruiters I interviewed told me a version of the same blunt thing: the job ad is frequently a weak artifact of an unfinished internal conversation. The role isn't defined as a business outcome. The problem isn't scoped. Tradeoffs aren't agreed on. So recruiting begins on a moving target, and once the process starts, requirements shift mid-funnel. From the outside, this reads as randomness. From the inside, it's uncertainty being managed in public. More applicants don't help here. If you don't know what good looks like, more options just mean more confusion.

When everything is AI-written, nothing is a signal

Once people believe the first filter is automated, the rational move is to write for the machine. Candidates tune CVs for ATS. They generate cover letters that "sound right", filling in all the keywords just to pass the filters. Recruiters get texts that feel generic and start distrusting all of it. Both sides become more defensive: candidates apply more because the feedback loop is dead, recruiters filter harder because the pile is unmanageable. The process becomes a conversation between tools, with humans doing cleanup on both ends.

Nobody has the mandate to stop the machine

Candidates can't stop. People are paying rent and trying to keep momentum. Recruiters are screaming, not screening. Hiring managers feel the cost of bad hires but don't own the tooling. Leadership optimizes for speed and risk reduction. Even when everyone agrees it's broken, the system keeps running because stopping it isn't an option anyone has.

The platforms profit from this without being the villain. More applications looks like growth. More engagement looks like success. When hiring gets harder, the platform becomes more necessary. The infrastructure does what it's rewarded for: activity, not accuracy.

I'm not anti-AI. I use it constantly. But we've built a system where AI makes the judgments and humans do the waiting. That's backwards. The fix isn't less AI. It's a different interface between people. One that rewards context over keywords.
