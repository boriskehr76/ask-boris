3. The Promised Land That Wasn't

Created on 2026-04-24 11:17

Published on 2026-04-24 11:23

I used to tell people UX design was a good career bet.

Good money. Remote-friendly. Creative but structured. No computer science degree required. Lot of job opportunities. For a while, the market rewarded that pitch. New UX educations, bootcamps and online courses popped up all over the place. The promised land got crowded fast.

A market that built more supply than it could absorb

Junior designers competed with mid-level candidates competed with seniors who had been laid off competed with career changers who had finished a bootcamp three months earlier. The pool of designers tripled while the amount of jobs stayed the same.

Naturally companies raised the bar. Eight years required experience, earlier the max, now became the floor. Just to keep the volume down.

As one recruiter said:

"If you write five years in the ad, you get applicants with three. Write eight, you get applicants with five. The numbers have lost meaning. It is an arms race with job requirements."

A.I. enters the stage

Design and creativity were seen as the only thing machines couldn't do. Until they did.

While established designers spent months debating whether AI was worth using, the newcomers embraced it immediately. AI let them punch above their weight with minimal effort. Generating visual concepts, producing wireframes that look polished, writing synthetic UX copy, producing case study text from rough notes — all became faster and cheaper.

The surface layer is what most hiring processes actually evaluate. A portfolio that looks sharp can now be produced by someone with three months of experience and the right prompts. The signal that hiring managers already struggled to read got noisier at exactly the moment the supply of designers peaked.

The portfolio that used to be a strong signal about your skills and thinking could now be fabricated and generated. Spotting the difference between something real — with genuine afterthoughts and real-world compromises — versus a well-generated portfolio, became really hard.

Only in later stages, when you actually need to present your cases, describe your why, all the failed attempts, twists and turns during the project, only then does it become obvious if there is something behind the shiny artifacts.

This problem is not hard — it is wicked.

A hard problem has a solution if you work at it long enough. A wicked problem is one where every attempted fix shifts the burden somewhere else and creates a new problem downstream. It is when a system is part of other systems, where problems and solutions are intertwined.

There is no single solution. Especially when there are other factors that nobody can foresee or control — wars, economic tendencies, the speed at which AI can help or replace designers, companies taking safe bets, replacing workers with AI, or stopping hiring juniors.

When looking backward, it all kind of makes sense: Good times when everybody needed designers led to schools and bootcamps rushing to fill the gap, led to a flooded market and companies raising the bar, candidates using AI to get through, requiring hiring managers to do the same to filter the avalanche of applications.

And here we are.

What the research showed

What surprised me the most from all the conversations was the agreement. Both sides described the same broken experience from opposite ends. Both want fewer, better interactions. Both feel trapped in a system they did not design and cannot unilaterally change.

The system optimizes for activity. Volume. Throughput. Not for matches.

AI is used as a crutch by both parties, not as a constructive tool that brings out the best on both sides. Both sides use it, but not well. More AI is the answer to nobody. Nor are more tests, more steps in the process, or harder requirements.

We need to find a way to make the process more transparent and more human. Leave the administration, tables, and numbers to the machines and focus on conversations and relations. We need to stop treating the hiring process as finding the right set of skills to fix a problem. Instead, treat it as the start of a relationship where a person joins a group with the intent to create something amazing together.

That gap between what the system rewards and what both sides actually want is the design problem hiding inside the market problem.
